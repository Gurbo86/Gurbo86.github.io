The Penguin Series.

By Leonel Maguet

For a long time, I’d been thinking about what to write, but today I realised that this is my blog and I’m going to write about whatever I feel like. This is a personal take on The Penguin, and it will contain spoilers, so if you haven’t seen it, you might not want to read it.

I’ve just watched the final episode of “The Penguin” and I have to say this miniseries (because, in my opinion, it should stay that way) makes the finished product excellent. From the character creation (villains, in this case), to the script, to the performances, especially those of Colin Farrell, Cristin Milioti, Deirdre O’Connell, and Rhenzy Feliz.

The Penguin starts with a comfortable Oswald Cobblepot in a Gotham City on fire (or, rather, underwater ). With a position in the mafia as a boss, running what seems to be his own operation, and waiting for an opportunity to take his next step up. Everything is running like a British train (on time and as it should) when something inside him pushes him into making a string of mistakes that will set all the events of the series in motion, but let’s not get ahead of ourselves.

In the opening minutes of the series, Oswald, in the middle of the night, walks into the Iceberg Lounge and breaks into the safe that belongs to Carmine Falcone, getting diamonds and blackmail material that will allow him to move forward in his search for power. But Alberto Falcone, none other than the son of the capo who died in The Batman, catches him red-handed. Faced with the complicated situation he’s in, he tries to navigate it with real expertise, trading money for power, handing the diamonds over to Alberto, until Alberto hits a raw nerve in his ego, and Oswald, seeing himself in that insult, proceeds to put three shots into his chest.

From that unfortunate event, Oswald goes on to do what he’s done his whole life: sweep the dirt under the rug and lie. But this time, by some twist of fate, he meets Víctor Aguilar, someone he gradually learns to see as a friend and ally on the path he has to walk through this miniseries.

Unfortunately for Oswald, his slip-up is no small thing, because the man he killed is none other than Sofia Falcone’s brother, a brother who believed in her and helped keep her sane during her time in Arkham Asylum. And it just so happens that Sofía has been recently released from the Asylum, so he has to erase the clues that would point to him as guilty of the crime.

That is simply the beginning of The Penguin: a simple, classic pitch, like any crime programme (not a superhero one) you might find in American primetime. What comes after this brief description is what makes it unique.

The series goes down a classic route in terms of its format, becoming raw; not because of the crime, nor because of the gangster life, nor because of the illusory idea that you have to give everything to win the crown, but rather by showing what it seems we all know and none of us dares to say. The top is full of broken people; the crown comes with its weight, and owning it is only an illusion we should question, whether it’s really worth it.

The series gives each character space to explain why they are the way they are, and what makes it so good is that, at no point, is it presented as a justification of their crimes, but rather as a way of giving us a sense of the motivations behind each character. We don’t have a Harleen Quinzel who justifies her wrongdoing because she was abused by the Joker; instead, for each character essential to the plot, we get almost an episode explaining where they came from, where we’re standing now, and where we’re going.

All of this is woven into the present-day events of the series so well that even the aesthetic choices feel extremely appropriate, and the high point of those decisions is the final episode. Some of these big choices come through in the decision to keep Oswald’s mother in her elderly form as the image that lives in his memory, as if she herself were re-evaluating her memories while living them.

The series can pass for a straightforward mob show. If we didn’t know about Batman, it could be a parallel thread in a season of The Sopranos (with more explosions and bigger traumas), but, at the end of the day, it’s just back-and-forth manoeuvring in which the underdog climbs his ladder towards mob glory. That is, until episode 8, where everything is resolved in a masterful way.

In the final episode, you find a lot of answers to many questions, both fictional and real.

In this film, the good ones die

As Attaque 77 said back in 2003: “No happy ending, in this film, the good ones die.” The Penguin isn’t a story with a happy ending, but it’s a perfect example of how you take a comic-book villain out of his stiff world of fiction and bring him into a story that’s closer to the “real world”. In this story, we’re shown an ambitious Oswald, with attitudes closer to noir crime fiction, only to turn him back into the cruel, unhinged villain he is in the comics in the final minute of the miniseries.

Because The Penguin only tells the origin story, in which fans will feel that several basic aspects of the villain are covered. The umbrella, given that his Cain-and-Abel crime happens on a night of heavy rain in Gotham. The suit, given his fascination with Top Hat, a film he will watch for the nth time that same night. And abandonment, replaced by a sickly relationship with his mother, which gets an excellent twist in the final episode of the series.

The character of Sofia Falcone, the antagonist of this series (and not a villain, because everyone is bad here), is one of the few you can empathise with, up to a point, in terms of what she does. A victim of a tyrannical father, locked up for ten years in Arkham, she returns to the world to find that her only anchor, her brother, has been killed by the person who ratted her out out of curiosity and, in part, sent her to that cruel prison. Only through her choice, the path of revenge, does she end up becoming just another one in the flock of sinners.

Only Víctor Aguilar, whom Oswald will gradually drag down the path of sin through innocence, is the character any decent person can find something to empathise with, and who, sadly, will pay the price for his innocence and good faith at the hands of his new fraternal figure.

The Godfather’s rise

In episode 6 of The Penguin, we see Cobblepot manage to unite the other gangs (the Triads, the Sullivan Crime Family, Burnley Town Massive, the Odessa Mob, and The Low Boys) into a united front against the Maroni/Gigante alliance.

It’s important to highlight that the gangs’ unity is consolidated mainly not by the leaders, but by those beneath them: their seconds-in-command. And the reason for that lies in the speech — who gives it and what it represents. Cobblepot’s speech is about how the most powerful family, the one that runs the rest of the gangs, has lost touch with the reality of the other crews. While Maroni/Gigante are comfortable and enjoy the riches of the business, the rest of the smaller gangs do the work that keeps them in those comforts.

And what reaches the seconds-in-command is how Cobblepot presents himself as one of them — those at the bottom — with the idea of taking them upwards; something he will end up doing for himself by the end of the series. The problem here is for the capos, who are in a position where they don’t quite understand (or don’t want to) the concerns of those below, and how that will end up working against them.

You can see this in the response from the Triads’ leader, Zao, who appeals to common sense, pointing to Oswald as a code-less liar — which he is — but he offers no solution to the situation Oz describes. For Zao, Oz is a danger, and Maroni/Gigante, even if he doesn’t like them, allows him to stay in his comfortable position. The contrast is his second-in-command, who connects with the speech, even though he knows Cobblepot is lying, because he lives what happens on the streets.

And at the end of this episode, Cobblepot does exactly that: he explains what the ordinary mobster feels when he has to sell the drug on a street corner — “drops” in the series — that make millions for Maroni/Gigante for a miserable margin, and earn their contempt, while they swim in the opulence of their neighbourhoods and mansions.

He explains how, from their ivory tower, Maroni/Gigante don’t know what’s happening beneath them, to the point that they don’t even know the names of the people they run, because they have someone in the middle who knows them on their behalf. And that middle layer, the heads of the five families, probably don’t know either, because for that they have a second-in-command who will take care of knowing it and doing the organisation’s dirty work.

And that’s why, at the start of episode 8, even though the pact from episode 6 is accepted by everyone, the heads of the five families are eliminated, because they belong to the old order, and the new order sees reality the way Cobblepot does.

A Miniseries

The Penguin is an eight-episode series, but, in my view, it should remain a miniseries. Víctor’s death at the end marks the high point of what the Penguin is, because it perfectly captures that villain who doesn’t hesitate for a second to eliminate anyone who becomes a vulnerability; although in this case, he doesn’t do it because Víctor is dead weight.

Víctor dies because he’s the closest person Cobblepot has after his own mother’s betrayal, and he can’t allow anyone to be close, because anyone close is likely to be a traitor or a weapon that can be used against him.

In this way, the miniseries would close an essential arc that would allow the character's future appearances, but any continuation would dull its shine. The series shouldn’t have a continuation; it should make way for other origin miniseries or development arcs scattered within it.

As the final cliffhangers, we have Selina Kyle (Catwoman), who appears at the end of the series as Sofia Falcone/Gigante’s half-sister (something that was already known from The Batman), as well as how Julian Rush, Sofia’s psychologist, will evolve, and what Sofia herself will become now that she understands what she wants to do with her life and will have plenty of time to think about how to do it in Arkham.
